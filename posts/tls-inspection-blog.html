The argument for TLS inspection is sound. Roughly 80% of malware now uses encrypted channels for C2 communication. An NGFW that can't see inside TLS is, effectively, a very expensive packet forwarder. If your IPS signatures, DLP rules, and URL categorization can't touch encrypted traffic, you've built your security stack on an assumption that the bad actors stopped using HTTPS — which they absolutely have not.

So you deploy SSL/TLS inspection. The firewall becomes a MITM — by design, with full administrative intent. It terminates the client's TLS session, decrypts the payload, inspects it, re-encrypts with a new session toward the server, and passes traffic along. The architecture diagram looks clean. The vendor says throughput is fine. The compliance team signs off.

What the architecture diagram doesn't show is what you broke in the process.

The Core Problem

You Are the Man in the Middle

TLS inspection is, technically and architecturally, a Man-in-the-Middle attack. The only difference between what your NGFW is doing and what an attacker would do is authorization — you've explicitly set it up. But the cryptographic implications are identical.

The client thinks it's talking to the server. It's not. It's talking to your firewall. The firewall is talking to the server. There are two separate TLS sessions, two separate certificate chains, two separate key negotiation processes. The "end-to-end" assurance that TLS is supposed to provide has been deliberately broken at the midpoint.

⚠ The Foundational Paradox

TLS inspection requires your firewall to present a certificate the client will trust — which means you've issued a sub-CA from your enterprise PKI, or you're running a rogue CA on a trusted anchor. From a PKI standpoint, that sub-CA can sign any certificate. Your NGFW is now a Certificate Authority. If that device is compromised, an attacker can issue legitimate-looking certificates for any domain — including your own.

Risk 1

Forward Secrecy: The Quiet Casualty

Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS) is one of TLS's most important properties. With PFS — implemented via ephemeral Diffie-Hellman (DHE/ECDHE) key exchange — each session generates a fresh set of session keys. Capture the traffic today, compromise the server's private key tomorrow: it doesn't matter. You cannot retroactively decrypt sessions protected by PFS because the session keys were never stored and were never derivable from the long-term private key.

This guarantee exists between the client and the server. When you insert a TLS inspection device, you now have two sessions. PFS on the client-to-NGFW leg is entirely dependent on what your firewall does. And here is where most deployments quietly break the model:

Cipher suite negotiation at TLS inspection point
# What the server offered (modern, FS-capable) TLS_AES_256_GCM_SHA384 ← TLS 1.3, FS inherent TLS_CHACHA20_POLY1305_SHA256 ← TLS 1.3, FS inherent # What your 3-year-old NGFW actually negotiated toward the client TLS_RSA_WITH_AES_256_CBC_SHA256 ← RSA key exchange, NO FS TLS_RSA_WITH_AES_128_CBC_SHA ← RSA key exchange, NO FS # Result: All client traffic re-encrypted with static RSA key. # Capture the NGFW private key → decrypt ALL historical sessions. FS guarantee: SILENTLY ELIMINATED

This happens because TLS inspection appliances — especially older NGFW versions — have their own cipher suite limitations. They may not support ECDHE on the client-facing leg, or they downgrade to RSA for performance reasons. The logs show TLS. The traffic looks encrypted. But every session is retroactively decryptable with a single key compromise.

Check your NGFW's TLS inspection cipher policy right now. If it includes any TLS_RSA_* cipher suite on the client-facing leg, you've quietly broken forward secrecy for all inspected traffic.

Risk 2

Certificate Pinning: Silent Application Breakage

Certificate pinning is a security mechanism where an application hard-codes the expected certificate (or its public key hash) for a specific server. When the connection is established, the application checks the presented certificate against its pin. If it doesn't match — even if the cert is otherwise valid and trusted — the connection is rejected.

Your NGFW is presenting its own certificate, signed by your sub-CA. The pin doesn't match. The application breaks.

⚡ The Problem Is You Don't Know It's Happening

Pinning failures are frequently opaque. The app doesn't say "TLS inspection detected." It says "connection failed," "service unavailable," or — if you're lucky — logs an SSL error. Certificate pinning is widely used in financial apps, MDM agents, EDR sensors, cloud backup clients, and enterprise SaaS products. In a large enterprise, you likely have dozens of pinned applications. Your NGFW's bypass list is probably missing half of them.

Common categories that break silently under TLS inspection:

Risk 3

OCSP Stapling Failures and Revocation Blindness

Online Certificate Status Protocol (OCSP) stapling is a mechanism where the server pre-fetches its own certificate revocation status from the CA and includes it ("staples" it) in the TLS handshake. This means the client gets revocation status without having to make a separate OCSP request — faster, more private, and more reliable.

When your NGFW intercepts, it terminates the server's TLS session and creates a new one toward the client. The OCSP staple from the real server certificate doesn't carry across — the NGFW is presenting its own forged certificate, not the server's. The staple is lost.

What happens next depends on your client configuration:

Soft-fail OCSP (default)
Client checks for OCSP response, doesn't get one, shrugs and continues. You've lost revocation checking entirely. A revoked server cert looks the same as a valid one.
Hard-fail OCSP
Client requires OCSP response and rejects connections without one. More secure — but your TLS inspection deployment will break these connections systematically until your NGFW is configured to perform and forward OCSP responses itself.

The security implication of soft-fail is severe: certificate revocation becomes meaningless for inspected traffic. A compromised certificate that has been revoked at the CA will still be accepted by clients behind your inspection device, because neither the staple nor the fallback OCSP lookup is being properly handled.

Risk 4

TLS 1.3 Was Deliberately Designed to Resist You

TLS 1.3 removed a significant number of features that made inspection easier — not by accident, but by deliberate design by the IETF. The security community understood that middlebox inspection was being used not just by enterprise firewalls but also by nation-state adversaries and malicious ISPs, and they tightened the protocol accordingly.

What changed in TLS 1.3 that directly impacts inspection:

  • 01
    Encrypted SNI (ESNI) / Encrypted Client Hello (ECH)

    The Server Name Indication — which tells the server (and any middlebox) which hostname the client wants — is now encrypted in TLS 1.3 with ECH. Your NGFW can't read the SNI to decide whether to inspect the session before it's already established. URL-based bypass decisions become impossible.

  • 02
    0-RTT (Zero Round Trip Time) resumption

    0-RTT allows clients to send application data in the first message, before the handshake completes. Inspection devices that haven't processed the full handshake can't inspect 0-RTT data. Depending on your NGFW, these early data packets either bypass inspection or cause the session to fail.

  • 03
    Removal of static RSA key exchange

    TLS 1.3 mandates ephemeral key exchange. There is no longer a static server private key that a network appliance can be pre-loaded with to passively decrypt traffic. Every session requires active participation in the key exchange — which means passive decryption taps are dead, and only active MITM (your current approach) remains viable.

  • 04
    Certificate transparency expectations

    Browsers increasingly enforce Certificate Transparency (CT) log requirements. Your NGFW's sub-CA-signed certificates are not in any public CT log. Chrome and Firefox may generate visible warnings or hard failures for CT-non-compliant certificates, depending on the site's SCT requirements.

The IETF didn't make TLS 1.3 harder to inspect accidentally. They did it on purpose. Your inspection architecture is working against the direction the protocol is deliberately heading.

Risk 5

Compliance and Legal Exposure

Decrypting user traffic creates data handling obligations that most organizations haven't thought through carefully. The inspection device is processing plaintext — usernames, passwords, session tokens, personal communications, health information, financial data — even if only transiently and only for inspection purposes.

In regulated environments, the relevant frameworks have explicit requirements:

⚖ UAE / Regional Compliance Context

Under the UAE Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL) and the NCA's Essential Cybersecurity Controls, interception of communications — including network inspection — may require explicit data processing records, data minimization justification, and in some interpretations, notification to data subjects. Blanket TLS inspection of all user traffic without documented justification, scope limitation, and bypass for personal/banking sessions is a compliance risk, not just an operational one. The same applies under GDPR for any entity handling EU data subjects' traffic.

Architecture

Doing TLS Inspection Right

The answer isn't to remove TLS inspection. It's to deploy it with full awareness of what it breaks and a compensating control for each failure mode.

The non-negotiable controls for any mature TLS inspection deployment:

  • 01
    ECDHE-only on the client-facing leg — no exceptions

    Audit your NGFW's cipher policy for the decrypted-to-client session. Disable all TLS_RSA_* suites. If your appliance can't enforce ECDHE on the client leg, it's not fit for TLS inspection in a PFS-required environment.

  • 02
    Maintain and actively update the bypass list

    Build the bypass list from observed pinning failures + known-pinned app categories + regulated data categories. Review quarterly. Assign ownership — this list decays fast as apps update their pinning. Automation via category feeds (Palo Alto EDL, Fortinet ISDB) helps but doesn't replace manual curation.

  • 03
    Sub-CA in a hardware HSM, name-constrained

    The sub-CA that signs inspection certificates must live in an HSM — never exported, never on the NGFW's local storage. Apply X.509 name constraints so it can only sign certificates for your internal domain namespace. A compromised NGFW should not yield a sub-CA capable of signing *.google.com.

  • 04
    Configure OCSP relay on the inspection device

    Your NGFW must fetch OCSP responses for the real server certificate and reconstruct stapling toward the client. Most enterprise NGFWs support this — it's frequently not configured. Without it, revocation checking is silently disabled for all inspected sessions.

  • 05
    Log metadata only — never buffer plaintext

    The inspection device sees plaintext transiently. Ensure it logs session metadata (source, destination, category, policy action, file hashes) — never the payload. Payload logging creates a massive sensitive data store that is itself a breach target and a compliance liability.

  • 06
    Document the inspection scope formally

    Which traffic is inspected, why, for what security purpose, and what bypass categories exist. This document is your defence in a regulatory audit or a data subject complaint. "We inspect everything" is not a defensible position under PDPL or GDPR without documented justification and proportionality assessment.

TLS inspection is not optional in an environment where the majority of malware exfiltrates over HTTPS and DLP rules need to see payload to fire. The choice isn't whether to inspect — it's whether you've accounted for what the inspection breaks.

Most deployments haven't. The forward secrecy gap is invisible in logs. The pinning failures look like app bugs. The OCSP issue is never noticed because soft-fail means traffic continues. The TLS 1.3 limitations are patched around in ways that quietly downgrade security. Everything looks like it's working, and some of it quietly isn't.

The NGFW vendor's documentation tells you how to enable inspection. It rarely tells you what you've traded away to do it. That gap is yours to close.
