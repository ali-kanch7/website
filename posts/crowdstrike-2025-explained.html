CrowdStrike's 2025 Threat Hunting Report

The Hackers Aren't Breaking In.
They're Getting Let In.

CrowdStrike's analysts spent a year watching real intrusions unfold inside real companies. The headline finding: most of the scariest ones didn't involve a single piece of malware. Someone just walked through the front door, because someone else handed them the keys.

Who's Who
Every group CrowdStrike tracks gets a two-word codename, and the second word isn't random. Tap one.

Somewhere in the last twelve months, a company's help desk got a phone call from someone who sounded exactly like a stressed employee locked out of their account. Same cadence, same urgency, maybe even the right internal jargon. The help desk did what help desks are trained to do: they helped. A password got reset. Nobody broke down a door, because nobody had to. They were buzzed in.

That single moment — not a virus, not a clever piece of code, just a phone call that worked — is the real story buried inside CrowdStrike's latest Threat Hunting Report. It's a dense, data-heavy document built for security teams, full of operational codenames and statistics that mean a lot more once you know what they're pointing at. Strip away the formatting and what's underneath is closer to a true-crime account of how people actually get conned at scale in 2025 — and it turns out the cons are getting good.

The naming system, once you see it, is kind of brilliant

CrowdStrike tracks dozens of hacking groups without ever knowing most of their real names. What they have instead is behavior — the same group tends to reuse the same tools, chase the same kind of victims, and operate out of roughly the same part of the world. So each group gets a two-word codename, and the second word isn't decorative. PANDA means a China-linked group. SPIDER means financially motivated criminals working for themselves rather than any government. CHOLLIMA points to North Korea. KITTEN is Iran, BEAR is Russia, JACKAL is hacktivism rather than a nation at all. The first word is just the individual's nickname — GENESIS, MURKY, SCATTERED — like a case file label.

Once that clicks, the report stops reading like alphabet soup and starts reading like a cast list. Tap a few names above and the cast starts to take shape: a patient telecom-focused operation here, a smash-and-grab ransomware crew there, a state-run fraud scheme dressed up as a job applicant.

The scariest number in the whole report has nothing to do with malware

For most people, hacking still looks like the movies — a hooded figure, a wall of green text, some dramatic countdown. CrowdStrike's numbers tell a quieter story.

81%
of serious intrusions involved no malware whatsoever. No infected file, nothing for antivirus software to flag. The intruder simply logged in with a real password and started working — often indistinguishable, at first glance, from an actual employee having an ordinary day.

This is the report's real thesis, and it explains why "threat hunting" exists as a job at all. If an attacker never trips a malware signature, no automated system rings a bell. Someone has to go looking — comparing what a login is doing against what that account normally does, noticing the file access pattern that's slightly off, catching the login from a country that account has never touched before. It's less digital forensics and more a very patient form of people-watching, done at the scale of an entire company's network.

What actually happens, start to finish

CrowdStrike calls a hands-on, human-driven break-in an "interactive intrusion" — the distinction being that nobody's running automated malware and walking away; an actual person is at a keyboard somewhere, adapting in real time. What stands out is how unremarkable each step looks in isolation. There's no moment that resembles breaking a lock. That's exactly the problem — and exactly why a piece of software looking for "broken locks" keeps missing people who were simply handed a key.

Four things that actually changed this year

Annual threat reports tend to drown good information in statistics nobody outside the field needs. A few numbers from this one are worth holding onto, because each marks a real shift rather than a minor wobble:

  • 1
    Phone scams are up 442%

    Calling a help desk and pretending to be a locked-out employee — or calling an employee while pretending to be the help desk — has become one of the single most effective ways in. It works because it targets a person trained to be helpful, not a piece of infrastructure trained to say no.

  • 2
    Cloud break-ins more than doubled

    As companies moved more of their actual work into Microsoft 365, Google Workspace, and similar hosted services, attackers simply followed the work. The office moved online; so did the burglars.

  • 3
    One crew can now go from "in" to ransomware in a single day

    SCATTERED SPIDER, a financially driven group with no government backing, has compressed what used to take weeks into about 24 hours — first access to fully locked-up systems and a ransom note.

  • 4
    A North Korea-linked group got hired — legitimately — at 320+ companies

    Not through any technical exploit. Through job applications, polished by AI, sometimes backed by real-time deepfake video during interviews. More on this below, because it's the part of the report that's genuinely hard to stop thinking about.

    • The job interview that wasn't one

    If there's a single case study built to make a non-technical reader sit up, it's this one — and tellingly, it has almost nothing to do with computers in the way people usually imagine hacking.

    FAMOUS CHOLLIMA North Korea-linked

    Operatives linked to this group apply for ordinary remote tech jobs. The résumé is AI-polished. The video interview, according to CrowdStrike's findings, sometimes runs through real-time deepfake software that swaps the applicant's actual face and voice for a more palatable one. They interview well. They get hired.

    From there, they're simply an employee — laptop, login, benefits, the works — quietly funneling a salary back to the regime, or using legitimate internal access for something closer to espionage. CrowdStrike counted this happening at over 320 companies in the past year, a number that's grown 220% since the year before.

    There's no firewall setting that catches this. The fix lives in hiring, not IT — video calls that can't be filtered around, identity checks that go further than a résumé and a Zoom call, an HR process built with the knowledge that the applicant on the other end of the call might not be who the screen says they are.

    Who's actually getting picked on

    It's tempting to assume only banks and governments are worth a determined attacker's time. The sector data says otherwise.

    Technology
    The most targeted sector of all, and for a practical reason: a foothold inside one tech company often opens a path into every other company that relies on its software.
    Consulting
    Firms with remote access into dozens of client networks at once are, from an attacker's perspective, a master key wearing a suit.
    Government
    Up 71% in overall intrusions, with deliberately targeted attacks climbing even faster — a 185% jump — as state-linked groups keep government high on the list.
    Telecom
    GLACIAL PANDA's whole approach is patience — slow, quiet infiltration of phone and internet providers, the kind of access that eventually means visibility into millions of people's call records.

    The common thread isn't fame. It's leverage. A mid-sized consulting firm with open doors into forty client networks is, in practice, a far more interesting target than a household name with one network and a strong security team standing in front of it.

    Two more ways in, neither of them dramatic

    GENESIS PANDA China-linked

    Rather than chase individual applications one at a time, this group goes straight for the control panel companies use to run their cloud infrastructure — the master settings page behind an entire Amazon Web Services or Microsoft Azure setup. Win there, and almost everything else is just a few clicks away.

    MURKY PANDA China-linked

    This one skips the front door of the target entirely and walks in through a vendor the target already trusts. If a company has given a contractor or software provider a standing connection into its systems, MURKY PANDA's bet is that nobody's double-checking traffic coming through a door that's already been propped open for years.

    Almost nothing in this report is really about code. It's about doors that were left open on purpose, for good reasons, by people who never expected anyone to notice.

    None of this requires becoming a security expert to understand, and that's sort of the point. The report's real argument travels well outside IT departments: the weakest moment in almost any system is the instant one person decides to trust another. A help desk trusting a familiar-sounding voice. A hiring manager trusting a polished résumé and a clear video call. A company trusting a vendor's access without asking when it was last reviewed.

    The tools in this report — cloud control panels, malware-free break-ins, AI-assisted job applications — sound new. The thing being exploited underneath all of it is not. It's just moving faster, and wearing a better disguise, than it used to.

    The attackers didn't get better at breaking in.
    They got better at being believed.
