Talking to It Properly: Prompting as a Real Skill

Two people ask the same model the same question and get answers a full grade apart in usefulness — same weights, same training, same day. The difference was never the model. It was what they typed first.

Same task, same model — both prompts, side by side
Vague prompt
"Write something about our firewall policy."
Produces a generic, encyclopedia-style overview of what a firewall policy generally covers — inbound/outbound rules, logging, review cycles — written for no audience in particular, in no particular format, at a length the model picked for you.
Specific prompt
"Write a 200-word firewall change policy summary for non-technical executives, explaining why change requests need a 48-hour review window. No jargon. End with one sentence on business risk."
Produces an executive-ready paragraph at the right length, in the right register, that actually answers why the 48-hour window exists — because every decision that mattered was already made by you, not left for the model to guess.

Picture a help desk ticket. Someone writes "the network is slow, fix it" and walks away. Nobody hands that to a network engineer and expects a clean resolution — not because the engineer is incapable, but because the sentence doesn't contain enough to act on. Which segment, which hours, which symptom, since when. The engineer either has to go fetch all of that themselves, or guess, and a guess is exactly as likely to be wrong as the engineer's priors happen to be unlucky that day.

A language model is in that engineer's position on every single request, except it can't walk over and ask a follow-up unless you let it. It will fill every gap you leave with a plausible guess, instantly, with no signal to you that it was guessing at all. That's the entire reason prompting is a real, learnable skill rather than a knack for being polite to a machine — and it's worth being precise about what it actually is, because "be more specific" undersells how mechanical the effect actually is.

01
Two jobs, two different prompts

System prompt vs. user prompt — who's talking to whom

Every conversation with a model is actually built from at least two layers of instruction, even when you only see one. The system prompt is set up in advance — by the company building the product, by a developer wiring the model into an app, sometimes by you, if the interface allows it — and it defines the role and ground rules for the whole conversation: who the model is meant to be, what tone to use, what it should never do. The user prompt is the message you actually type, turn by turn. The model treats both as instructions, but the system prompt sits underneath everything, shaping how every user prompt gets interpreted, the way a job description shapes how an employee reads every individual request that lands on their desk.

This is worth knowing for a very practical reason: if a chatbot embedded in some product behaves strangely — refuses something reasonable, stays oddly formal, won't discuss a certain topic — that's frequently the system prompt doing exactly what it was told to do, not the underlying model being incapable. Two different products can run the identical model and behave like entirely different assistants, purely because of what's written in that invisible first layer.

02
Showing vs. telling

Zero-shot and few-shot — examples are instructions too

Zero-shot prompting is just asking directly, with no examples — "summarize this incident report in three bullet points." It works fine for tasks the model has seen described a thousand ways during training. Few-shot prompting hands the model one or more worked examples before the real request, and it's a different kind of lever entirely: instead of describing the format you want, you demonstrate it, and the model pattern-matches to your demonstration rather than to its own default assumptions about what "a summary" should look like.

The difference shows up constantly in anything with a house style. Ask a model cold to "write a security advisory," and it will reach for whatever an advisory looks like on average across its training data — a reasonable, generic shape. Show it one real advisory you've already written, in your own structure, with your own severity labels, and it will match that structure far more reliably than any paragraph of description could achieve. An example is worth several sentences of instruction, because it removes ambiguity a sentence can't.

03
The phrase that measurably changes accuracy

Chain-of-thought — making the model show its working

Chain-of-thought prompting asks the model to lay out intermediate reasoning steps before giving a final answer, rather than jumping straight to a conclusion. The original 2022 research demonstrating this — Wei et al., at Google — found it dramatically improved accuracy on multi-step math and logic problems compared to asking for the answer directly. A follow-up paper the same year found something almost comic in its simplicity: you don't even need worked examples to get most of the benefit. Appending the literal phrase "let's think step by step" to an otherwise plain question measurably improved results across a wide range of reasoning tasks. That's now called zero-shot chain-of-thought, and it remains one of the highest-leverage four words you can add to a prompt.

Why does this work at all? Part 2 covered how a model generates one token at a time, each new token conditioned on everything generated so far, including its own prior output. Force it to write out "first, second, then, therefore" before the final answer, and each of those intermediate tokens becomes part of the context the next token is predicted from — effectively giving the model room to build toward a correct answer step by step, rather than having to leap there in a single forward pass. It isn't reasoning in the human sense of deliberating before speaking. It's closer to thinking out loud being more reliable than thinking silently, because every word said out loud becomes something the next word can build on.

Worth sitting with

This is also the underlying idea behind what labs now call "extended thinking" or "reasoning models" — Claude's extended thinking, OpenAI's o-series, and others. Rather than relying on you to ask for step-by-step reasoning, these models are trained to generate a private scratchpad of reasoning automatically before producing a visible answer, with the depth of that scratchpad often adjustable. It's the same underlying mechanic as "let's think step by step," institutionalized into the model's default behavior rather than left to the person typing the prompt.

04
The one rule that outperforms every trick

Specificity beats cleverness

It's tempting to think good prompting is about finding the right magic phrase — some clever incantation that unlocks a smarter mode. Almost none of the actual gap between a mediocre prompt and a good one comes from cleverness. It comes from removing ambiguity the model would otherwise have to resolve by guessing.

"Make it more professional" hands the model your subjective standard with no anchor for what "professional" means to you specifically — formal vocabulary, shorter sentences, no contractions, all three. "Summarize this" doesn't say how long, for whom, or what to do with technical detail. Each of those vague instructions has many reasonable interpretations, and the model will quietly pick one rather than flag the ambiguity back to you — because nothing in how it was trained rewards stopping to ask. A specific version closes those gaps before they open: target length, intended audience, what to keep versus cut, what format the output should take. None of that is clever. It's just not leaving decisions on the table for the model to make on your behalf, invisibly.

A vague prompt isn't a shorter version of a good prompt. It's a good prompt with several decisions quietly handed to the model instead of made by you.
05
Where it goes wrong in practice

Failure patterns worth recognizing

A handful of failure shapes account for most disappointing outputs, and they're worth being able to name on sight, the way you'd recognize a misconfigured ACL faster the tenth time than the first.

→
UnderspecificationNo target format, length, or audience given — the model picks plausible defaults, which is rarely what you actually wanted.
→
Buried instructionsThe actual ask is on line one of a long paste, with the real constraint mentioned almost as an afterthought near the end — easy for a human to miss too, and the model has the same problem.
→
Conflicting instructions"Keep it brief" alongside "cover every edge case in detail" — the model has to silently pick a winner, and it won't tell you which one it picked.
→
Assuming memory that isn't thereReferring back to something from much earlier in a long conversation that's since scrolled past the effective attention of the model — more on why this happens in Part 2's note on context windows.

Notice that none of these are about the model being insufficiently powerful. They're about information that existed only in your head, never making it into the text the model actually had access to. The fix for all four is structurally the same: say the thing you were assuming, out loud, in the prompt.

06
Closing the loop from Part 2

What's actually happening as it answers you

Part 2 described training as a finished process by the time you start typing. Generation is the live part, and it's worth seeing once, plainly. The model doesn't draft a full reply and then send it. It predicts a probability distribution over its entire vocabulary for what the single next token should be, samples one token from that distribution, appends it to everything generated so far, and repeats — one token, then the next, then the next, each one conditioned on every token that came before it, including its own. This is why a response streams in piece by piece rather than appearing whole, and it's not an interface choice. It's the literal order in which the words come into existence.

A setting called temperature controls how that single-token choice gets made. At low temperature, the model leans hard toward whatever token it judged most probable — consistent, a little safe, the same answer nearly every time you ask. Push temperature higher and it more readily picks less-probable tokens, which reads as more varied or "creative" phrasing, right up until it's pushed too far and the output stops making sense entirely. Most everyday chat use sits in a comfortable middle of that range without you ever touching the dial — but it's worth knowing the dial exists, because it explains why the same question asked twice can come back worded differently, even with nothing else changed.

07
Where Part 4 picks up

A good prompt still only gets you one answer

Everything in this part assumes a single exchange: you ask, the model answers, done. That covers most of what a chatbot is. It does not cover a system that can check a calendar, search a document, run code, or take five separate steps to get you a correct answer instead of one polished-sounding guess. That requires the model to do something fundamentally different from completing a sentence — to act, observe what happened, and decide what to do next. That shift, from chatbot to agent, is where Part 4 starts.

The help desk ticket from the top of this page gets resolved the same way either system actually works: more information, given earlier, removes guesswork later. A model with a vague prompt and a network engineer with a vague ticket fail for the identical reason. Neither one was missing capability. Both were missing the information that was sitting, unsent, in someone's head.

The model was never the bottleneck. The sentence you typed first was.
