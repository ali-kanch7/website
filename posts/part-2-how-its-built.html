Part 2 of 6

How a Model Like This Actually Gets Built

Part 1 explained how a network learns to predict the next word. That alone gets you a very fluent autocomplete, not an assistant. Something else has to happen in between — and the gap between those two things is wider, and stranger, than most explanations let on.

Drag through the build — same model, three stages

Ask a model fresh out of pretraining a question — "what's the capital of the UAE?" — and there's a real chance it doesn't answer you at all. It might continue your sentence with three more questions in the same style, the way autocomplete on your phone keeps suggesting words rather than holding a conversation. That's not a bug. It's doing exactly the job it was trained for, which was never "answer questions." It was "predict what text comes next," and a quiz full of unanswered questions is, statistically, a very normal thing to find on the internet.

Getting from that raw, twitchy text-predictor to something that behaves like an assistant takes two more deliberate stages, built on top of the first. Each one reshapes the model's behavior without changing what it fundamentally is underneath: still the same stack of weights from Part 1, just nudged, again and again, toward usefulness.

01
Stage one, and the bill that comes with it

Pretraining: reading almost everything, learning almost nothing on purpose

Pretraining is the stage from Part 1: feed the network a colossal pile of text — book archives, filtered web pages, code repositories, forums — and have it repeatedly guess the next token, get corrected, guess again. There is no teacher curating right answers here, no rulebook. The "training signal" is just the text itself; every sentence that already exists on the internet is simultaneously the question and the answer key.

This stage is also where almost the entire training budget goes, and the dollar figures are worth sitting with because they explain a lot of what comes later. The original 2017 transformer cost roughly $900 to train. GPT-3, three years later, ran somewhere between $500K and $4.6M depending on hardware. GPT-4, by 2023, cost an estimated $78–100M in compute alone — Sam Altman has confirmed the figure exceeded $100 million — using on the order of 25,000 GPUs running for roughly three months. Google's Gemini Ultra reportedly cost over $190M. That's not a typo-prone exaggeration; it's three orders of magnitude in under a decade, and it's the single biggest reason frontier AI is currently a game only a handful of organizations can play at all.

Worth sitting with

A useful way to picture pretraining: it's less like teaching a student a subject and more like a single, vastly patient reader getting through a library larger than any human could read in a hundred lifetimes — not memorizing pages, but absorbing the statistical shape of how ideas, facts, and sentence structures tend to fit together. What comes out the other end can write fluent, plausible-sounding prose on almost anything. What it can't yet do is reliably distinguish "answer the question" from "continue the pattern."

02
Stage two, with a much smaller bill

Fine-tuning: teaching the shape of a conversation

Fine-tuning — more precisely, supervised fine-tuning, or SFT — takes the pretrained model and continues training it on a much smaller, much more carefully curated dataset: real examples of instructions paired with the kind of response a helpful assistant should give. Question, good answer. Request, good response. Thousands to millions of these pairs, instead of the entire internet. This is where a model first learns the basic shape of "you ask, I answer" rather than "continue whatever pattern is in front of me."

The cost difference between the two stages is enormous, and it matters for understanding who can build what. Pretraining a frontier model from scratch needs the kind of compute only a few labs in the world can afford. Fine-tuning an already-pretrained open model on a specific domain — say, customer support transcripts for a telecom company — can run anywhere from a few hundred to a few thousand dollars using parameter-efficient techniques. That asymmetry is the entire reason a wave of smaller companies can build specialized AI products without ever training a foundation model themselves.

03
Stage three, the one that's easy to underrate

RLHF: teaching taste, not facts

Fine-tuning teaches a model the shape of a good answer. It doesn't teach which of two reasonable-sounding answers a person would actually prefer — and "reasonable-sounding" leaves a lot of room for answers that are rude, evasive, dangerous, or just unhelpfully long-winded. That's the gap RLHF — reinforcement learning from human feedback — closes.

The mechanism is three steps. First, the fine-tuned model generates several different responses to the same prompt. Second, human reviewers rank those responses from best to worst — not by checking facts, but by judgment: which one would you actually want to receive. Third, those rankings train a separate reward model to predict human preference at scale, and that reward model is then used to nudge the original model's weights toward producing more of what scored well and less of what scored poorly, across millions of further examples. The reviewers never have to look at all of those millions; their judgment gets distilled into a model that can apply it tirelessly.

1
GenerateThe fine-tuned model produces several candidate answers to the same prompt.
2
RankHuman reviewers order the candidates by preference — not correctness, preference.
3
Reward modelThose rankings train a separate model to predict, at scale, what a human would likely prefer.
4
ReinforceThe reward model's scores are used to push the original model's weights toward higher-scoring behavior.

RLHF is also, not coincidentally, where most of what people experience as a model's "personality" — cautious here, blunt there, refusing some requests outright — actually gets shaped. None of it was written as a rule the way the phishing filter in Part 1 was. It was trained in, the same way everything else in this pipeline gets trained in: by adjusting weights until the observed behavior matches what was rewarded.

Pretraining teaches a model language. Fine-tuning teaches it conversation. RLHF teaches it taste.
04
Two ideas worth being precise about

Tokenizers and context windows — what the model actually sees

None of the three training stages above operate on words. They operate on tokens — and a token is rarely a whole word. A tokenizer breaks text into chunks using a method called byte-pair encoding: start with individual characters, repeatedly merge whatever pair of characters shows up most often in the training data, and keep merging until you've built a vocabulary of somewhere between 30,000 and 200,000 reusable chunks. Common English words usually end up as a single token. Rare words, unusual names, or anything in a less common script can get chopped into several oddly-sized pieces — which is part of why typing in some languages costs noticeably more per request than typing the same idea in English.

The context window is the total number of tokens a model can hold in view at once — your prompt, any system instructions, the conversation so far, and the response it's about to generate, all sharing one fixed budget. This number has grown almost absurdly fast: the original 2017 transformer effectively worked with sequences a few hundred tokens long; by 2026, context windows of a million tokens are standard across frontier models. But a bigger window isn't simply "more memory" in the way more RAM helps a computer. Independent research has repeatedly found that models pay closer attention to information near the start and end of a long context than to material buried in the middle — informally called the "lost in the middle" effect — so stuffing a context window full doesn't guarantee the model actually uses everything in it evenly.

05
The part nobody quite escapes

Why models hallucinate — and why it isn't a bug being patched out

"Hallucination" is the field's term for a model stating something false with total confidence — inventing a court case that doesn't exist, citing a statistic that was never published, describing a feature your software doesn't have. For years the working assumption was that this was a rough edge that better training would eventually sand away. Research from OpenAI published in September 2025 made a more uncomfortable case: hallucination is, in part, a predictable side effect of how these models are trained and graded in the first place.

The argument runs roughly like this. During training and on most evaluation benchmarks, a confident wrong answer and an honest "I don't know" are scored identically — both wrong, zero credit. A confident guess, on the other hand, has a real chance of being right and earning credit. Run that incentive over millions of training examples and you get a model that's statistically rewarded for guessing fluently rather than admitting uncertainty, in much the same way a student taking a multiple-choice exam with no penalty for guessing should always pick an answer rather than leave it blank. The model isn't "lying" in any sense that implies intent. It's continuing a pattern that looks like a confident, well-formed answer, because confident, well-formed answers are what scored well during training.

A model that says "I don't know" has never once been wrong in a way that costs it points — and under most current grading, that's exactly the problem.

This connects directly to something else worth naming clearly: a model's knowledge cutoff — the date after which its training data simply stops — is not a setting that can be nudged forward. It's baked into pretraining itself. A model trained on data through a certain month has no way of knowing what happened after that month unless something outside the model — a live web search, a connected database — hands it that information directly. Ask it about something recent without that help, and you've created exactly the conditions where a confident, fluent, wrong answer is most likely to show up.

06
The story changing fastest in 2026

Why bigger stopped being the obvious move

For most of the years following the 2017 transformer paper, the dominant strategy across the field was straightforward: bigger model, more data, more compute, better results, repeat. That pattern — known as a scaling law — held up well enough for long enough that it became something close to an article of faith. It's no longer the whole story, for a reason that's mostly economic rather than scientific: training and running a trillion-parameter model is staggeringly expensive, and most real-world use doesn't need a trillion parameters' worth of capability for every single request.

Two techniques have reshaped the field in response. The first is mixture-of-experts, or MoE — an architecture where a model is built from many specialized sub-networks, "experts," but only a handful of them actually activate for any given input. Google's early GLaM research found a 1.2-trillion-parameter MoE model could outperform a dense 175-billion-parameter model while using roughly half the computation per request, because most of those trillion parameters simply stay dormant unless a particular query actually needs them. It's the reason the largest open-weight models released in 2026 are MoE architectures — that scale would be close to unworkable as a fully dense network, where every parameter fires on every request.

The second is distillation: training a smaller "student" model to mimic the outputs of a larger "teacher" model, capturing much of the larger model's capability at a fraction of the size and inference cost. This is why a company can often deploy a smaller, cheaper, faster model for routine tasks and reserve the largest frontier model only for the hardest ones — DeepSeek's V3 model, trained for a widely-reported $5.6M, a small fraction of what comparable Western frontier models cost, became one of 2026's loudest proof points that clever engineering can substitute for raw compute spend.

Worth sitting with

None of this means scale stopped mattering. The current frontier models — still mostly trillion-parameter-class systems — remain the most capable available. What's changed is that "biggest" and "best choice for this job" have quietly become two different questions, and most production AI systems now route different requests to different-sized models rather than sending everything to the largest one by default.

07
Where Part 3 picks up

The model is built. Now it has to be told what to do

Everything in this part happens before you ever type a single word into a chat box. Pretraining, fine-tuning, and RLHF are all finished, baked into a fixed set of weights, by the time a model ships. What happens after that — the actual back-and-forth, the wording you choose, the difference between a vague request and a precise one — turns out to matter more than most people assume, and it's a real, learnable skill rather than a knack for being polite to a machine. That's Part 3.

Three stages, three different jobs, three wildly different price tags — and a model you talk to has all three baked into it before it ever sees your first message. None of that pipeline guarantees the model is right. It only guarantees the model sounds like it might be, which is a different thing, and worth remembering every time an answer arrives a little too smoothly.

A model that's never been wrong without sounding confident isn't trustworthy. It's just well trained. Those aren't the same compliment.
