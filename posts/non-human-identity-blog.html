Every Zero Trust conversation starts with the same picture: a user, an identity provider, an MFA prompt, a device posture check, a conditional access policy. The human identity is verified, the session is granted, and the controls are logged. It's a model that has improved enterprise security measurably.

Now open your Active Directory and filter for accounts whose names start with svc_. Look at when their passwords were last changed. Look at their group memberships. Look at who their manager is in the directory.

This is the other identity problem — and it's larger, older, and more dangerous than the human one. Non-human identities now outnumber human identities in most enterprise environments by a wide margin. They authenticate constantly, carry significant privilege, and operate completely outside the MFA-and-posture-check model that Zero Trust was built around. They are the gap in your program that attackers know about and practitioners rarely discuss.

The Landscape

The category is broader than most teams realise. Non-human identities include anything that authenticates to a system without a person directly behind the keyboard at that moment:

Why They're Dangerous

The Properties That Make Non-Human Identities a Threat Vector

It's not that service accounts are inherently insecure. It's that the properties necessary for them to do their jobs make them structurally resistant to every control in the standard security toolkit.

They Cannot Use MFA

Multi-factor authentication requires an out-of-band channel — a phone, a hardware token, an authenticator app. A service account that runs a nightly database backup job at 2 AM cannot receive an Authenticator push. The options are either exempt it from MFA entirely (the universal choice) or break the automation. Every enterprise chooses the former. This means your MFA policy, which covers 100% of human logins, covers 0% of service account authentications.

An attacker who compromises a service account credential faces no second factor. They authenticate exactly as the legitimate service does — same IP range (probably a server subnet, not geo-anomalous), same account name, same time of day if they're patient. The authentication logs look identical to legitimate activity.

Nobody Owns Them

Service accounts are created for a project, an application, a vendor integration. The engineer who created it moves to another team. The application gets rewritten. The vendor changes their integration method. The original purpose evaporates. The account remains, active, with whatever privilege it was granted on day one.

⚠ The Orphan Account Problem

Studies consistently find that 30–50% of service accounts in mature enterprise environments have no identifiable owner in any CMDB, ITSM, or directory attribute. These accounts have full credentials, active passwords, and ongoing authentication — with no human responsible for reviewing or revoking them. They are permanently unattended privileged access.

They're Over-Privileged by Design

When a service account is provisioned, the path of least resistance is to give it enough privilege to definitely work — which in practice means Domain Admin, local admin on all servers, or a database role with full access to every schema. Scoping permissions to exactly what the service needs requires knowing exactly what the service does, which requires documentation that usually doesn't exist, which requires time that the project didn't have.

The result: a service account running a file synchronisation job has Domain Admin rights. Not because anyone decided it needed them — because nobody decided otherwise, and DA was the safe choice that wouldn't cause support tickets.

Architecture

What Zero Trust Actually Means for Machine Identities

The Zero Trust principles that apply to human identities — verify explicitly, use least privilege, assume breach — apply equally to non-human identities. The implementation is different because the mechanisms are different, but the conceptual model transfers directly.

The three phases aren't a one-time project. They form a continuous loop — and the monitoring phase frequently reveals new identities that need to re-enter the discover phase. Non-human identity sprawl is a living problem, not a one-time audit target.

The SPIFFE/SPIRE Model: Cryptographic Workload Identity

For workload-to-workload authentication — the service mesh, the microservices, the container-to-database call — the most robust current approach is the SPIFFE (Secure Production Identity Framework for Everyone) standard, implemented via SPIRE.

The principle: every workload gets a cryptographically attested identity — a SPIFFE Verifiable Identity Document (SVID), essentially a short-lived X.509 certificate — issued by a trusted authority that has verified the workload's origin (which Kubernetes namespace, which AWS account, which VM image hash). Workloads authenticate to each other using mTLS with these certificates. No passwords. No static tokens. No API keys. The certificate is the identity, it's bound to the workload's provenance, and it expires in hours.

ℹ Why Short TTL Changes Everything

A credential that expires in one hour has a fundamentally different risk profile than one that never expires. Even if an attacker exfiltrates the SVID, they have at most 60 minutes to use it before it's worthless. Compare that to a service account password last rotated in 2019: the attacker has effectively permanent access. TTL is the most impactful single variable in non-human identity security.

Implementation

Building the Program: Where to Start

The gap between "we have a PAM tool" and "we have Zero Trust for non-human identities" is significant. Most enterprises are somewhere between the two. The path forward is phased — and the first phase is the one most teams skip:

  • Inventory everything before touching anything

    Run a full AD audit: every account with a service account naming convention, every account with a non-interactive login type, every account with no manager attribute, every account with a password over 365 days old. Run secret scanning across your repos (GitHub Advanced Security, GitGuardian, Trufflesecurity). Export your certificate inventory. Map your Kubernetes service accounts. You will find things that surprise you.

  • Assign an owner to every identity — make ownerlessness a policy violation

    Every service account, API token, and machine certificate needs a human owner who is responsible for reviewing and re-attesting it annually. Build this into your CMDB or PAM vault. Ownerless accounts are flagged automatically and escalated to the relevant team lead. Orphan accounts get disabled after 30 days without an owner claim.

  • Migrate static credentials to a vault — no new hardcoded secrets

    HashiCorp Vault, Azure Key Vault, AWS Secrets Manager, CyberArk Conjur. Pick one and make it the only acceptable place for a secret to live. Enforce this via pipeline controls — secret scanning in CI/CD gates, IaC scanning in Terraform/Bicep pipelines. New secrets are vault-fetched at runtime. Existing hardcoded secrets get a migration deadline, not a pass.

  • Enable Group Managed Service Accounts (gMSA) for Windows services

    For Windows service accounts running IIS, SQL Server, scheduled tasks, and background services, gMSA eliminates the password management problem entirely. The domain controller manages the password automatically on a rolling schedule (default 30 days). The application never knows the password. No human ever knows the password. No credential to steal.

  • Adopt managed identities for cloud workloads — eliminate API keys

    Azure Managed Identities, AWS IAM Roles for Service Accounts (IRSA), GCP Workload Identity Federation. For any cloud workload, these mechanisms assign a cryptographic identity at the infrastructure level — no API key to generate, rotate, or leak. An Azure VM with a Managed Identity authenticates to Key Vault by presenting its Azure-signed token. No credential exists to be stolen.

  • Monitor authentication behavior — establish baselines and alert on deviation

    A service account that authenticates from 2–3 AM on weekdays should never authenticate at 2 PM on a Sunday from a new source IP. SIEM rules for service account anomalies are straightforward to write and high-value to alert on — the signal-to-noise ratio is far better than for human account alerts because legitimate service account behavior is highly predictable and repetitive.

⚡ The Tooling Landscape

PAM / Vaulting: CyberArk (enterprise standard), HashiCorp Vault (developer-friendly, cloud-native), Delinea Secret Server. Workload identity: SPIRE (open source SPIFFE), Istio/Linkerd service mesh (mTLS), AWS IRSA, Azure Managed Identity. Secret scanning: GitHub Advanced Security, GitGuardian, Trufflesecurity Trufflehog. Certificate management: Venafi, Keyfactor, AWS Private CA. AD audit: Tenable.ad (formerly Alsid), BloodHound Enterprise, Microsoft Defender for Identity.

The Zero Trust maturity models all converge on the same gap when you push past the first tier: human identity is largely solved, or at least solvable. Non-human identity is the next frontier — and most programs haven't seriously started.

Every service account with a five-year-old password and Domain Admin rights is a standing invitation. Every API key hardcoded in a configuration file that was committed to a repository and never rotated is a credential waiting to be found. Every orphaned account with no owner is an identity with nobody watching it — which is exactly the kind of identity an attacker prefers to use.

The sophistication of your MFA deployment and your conditional access policies is real progress. It just doesn't apply to the identities that are already inside, already privileged, and already operating without any of those controls.
